# Parent Support Checklist for WAEC/JAMB Learners

Parents can support exam preparation without turning the home into a pressure zone.

## 1. Ask Better Questions

Instead of asking only, "Have you read?", ask:

- What topic did you study today?
- What did you understand better?
- What question did you get wrong?
- What will you revise tomorrow?

## 2. Help Them Plan

Ask the learner to show:

- Their subjects.
- Their weak topics.
- Their weekly timetable.
- Their practice scores.

## 3. Use AI Safely

AI can help explain, quiz, and review mistakes. It should not be used to copy assignments or avoid thinking.

House rules:

- Do not enter private family information into AI tools.
- Do not copy AI answers without understanding them.
- Use AI for explanations, practice, and feedback.
- Discuss confusing answers with a teacher or trusted adult.

## 4. Watch For Burnout

Signs of overload:

- Constant tiredness.
- Irritability.
- Avoiding study completely.
- Panic before practice tests.
- Sleeping too late repeatedly.

## 5. Encourage Practice, Not Perfection

Progress questions:

- Did your score improve?
- Did you understand your mistakes?
- Did you revise the weak topic again?

## Rule

Support the process. Do not create fear around the result.

## What Helpful Support Looks Like

Your role is not to become the teacher for every subject. Your role is to create the conditions that make serious preparation possible. That means helping with structure, protecting study time, encouraging honest review, and noticing when the child needs extra help.

A helpful home does not have to be silent all day. It should simply have predictable study periods. If your child knows that 7:00pm to 8:00pm is protected study time, it becomes easier to build a habit. If every day is random, preparation becomes random too.

Try to move from pressure questions to evidence questions. Instead of asking only, "Have you read?", ask what topic was studied, what questions were attempted, what mistake was discovered, and what will be revised next. These questions show whether learning is happening.

## How To Discuss AI Use At Home

AI can be useful, but children need boundaries. Set simple rules:

- Do not enter addresses, phone numbers, passwords, school portals, or private family information into AI tools.
- Do not ask AI to do schoolwork that you cannot explain.
- Use AI to explain, quiz, correct, and review.
- Check important answers with textbooks, teachers, or trusted materials.
- If AI gives a confusing answer, ask a teacher or responsible adult.

You can ask your child to show you the prompt they used. This is a powerful habit because it reveals whether they are asking AI to teach them or simply asking AI to give answers.

## Weekly Parent Review

Once a week, sit with your child for 15 to 20 minutes. Keep the conversation calm. Ask:

- Which subject improved this week?
- Which topic is still confusing?
- Which practice score changed?
- Which mistake keeps repeating?
- What support do you need from me next week?

Write down one action from the conversation. It may be buying past questions, arranging a quiet study block, speaking with a teacher, reducing distractions, or helping the child plan transport for lessons.

## How To Respond To Poor Scores

A poor score should start a diagnosis, not a fight. Ask what caused the score. Was the topic not understood? Was there not enough practice? Did the child rush? Was the paper harder than expected? Did anxiety affect performance?

When you understand the cause, you can respond wisely. If the issue is weak understanding, shouting will not teach the topic. If the issue is poor practice, the solution is a better routine. If the issue is anxiety, the child needs calm preparation and repeated exposure to timed questions.

## Encouragement Without Empty Promises

Do not promise that everything will be easy. Exams require effort. But you can remind your child that improvement is possible when preparation becomes specific and consistent.

Say things like:

- Let us identify the topic and work on it.
- Show me the mistake you found today.
- What is the next small step?
- I am more interested in your process than panic.
- We will look for help where you need it.

This kind of support builds confidence without pretending that results do not matter.

## Supporting Children Without Collecting Sensitive Information

For younger learners, you do not need to collect unnecessary personal information before giving support. Focus on learning behavior and progress. You can track subjects, topics, practice scores, mistake patterns, confidence level, and support needed. You do not need to turn the home into a data collection exercise.

If your child uses AI, remind them not to enter private details. They can say, "I am a student preparing for WAEC" without giving their full name, address, school login, or personal identifiers. Safety and learning can work together.

## When To Seek Extra Help

Sometimes home support and AI guidance are not enough. You may need extra help if your child keeps scoring very low after repeated practice, avoids one subject completely, cries or panics whenever the exam is mentioned, cannot explain basic ideas after several attempts, or has no quiet place to study.

Extra help may come from a teacher, lesson tutor, older sibling, classmate, school counselor, or structured learning programme. Do not wait until the final week before asking for help. Early support is usually calmer and more effective.

## A Simple Weekly Routine For Parents

Use this rhythm:

1. Monday: ask what topics are planned for the week.
2. Wednesday: ask what has been practiced and what mistake has appeared.
3. Friday: ask what topic needs weekend review.
4. Sunday: review the week calmly and agree on the next small step.

This routine keeps you involved without hovering over every study session. It also helps the child see that preparation is a process, not a sudden panic before results.

Your support should help your child become more honest, organized, and confident. That is the kind of preparation that lasts beyond one examination.
