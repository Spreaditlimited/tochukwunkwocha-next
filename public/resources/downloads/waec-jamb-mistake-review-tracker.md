# WAEC/JAMB Mistake Review Tracker

Many learners keep repeating the same mistakes because they only check the correct answer. This tracker helps you understand why you missed a question.

## How To Use It

After every practice session:

1. Write the question or topic.
2. Write your wrong answer.
3. Write the correct answer.
4. Ask AI to explain the mistake.
5. Write the lesson you learned.
6. Practice a similar question later.

## AI Review Prompt

> I am preparing for [WAEC/JAMB]. I got this question wrong: [QUESTION]. My answer was [ANSWER]. The correct answer is [CORRECT ANSWER]. Explain why my answer is wrong, show the correct reasoning, and give me two similar questions to practice.

## Mistake Types

Mark each mistake as one of these:

- I did not understand the topic.
- I misunderstood the question.
- I rushed.
- I forgot a formula or rule.
- I guessed.
- I made a calculation error.
- I knew it but panicked.

## Weekly Reflection

At the end of each week, answer:

- Which subject produced the most mistakes?
- Which mistake type appeared most often?
- Which topic should I revise first next week?
- What one habit should I change?

## Rule

A corrected mistake is progress. An ignored mistake will likely return in the exam.

## Why You Must Review Mistakes Properly

Practice questions are not useful only because they give you a score. They are useful because they show you what is happening inside your preparation. If you only check the correct answer and move on, you lose most of the benefit. The question you missed may be pointing to a weak concept, a careless reading habit, a formula problem, a time-pressure issue, or simple panic.

This tracker helps you slow down after practice. That slowdown is important. Many students rush from one set of questions to another because they want to feel productive. But productivity is not the same as improvement. Improvement happens when you notice the reason behind the wrong answer and correct it before the next practice session.

## The Five Mistake Categories

Use these categories when filling the tracker:

1. Concept mistake: you did not understand the topic well enough.
2. Memory mistake: you had studied it before but could not remember the rule, formula, definition, or fact.
3. Reading mistake: you misunderstood what the question was asking.
4. Process mistake: you knew the idea but followed the wrong step.
5. Pressure mistake: you rushed, panicked, guessed too early, or lost focus.

Each category requires a different response. A concept mistake needs explanation. A memory mistake needs spaced revision. A reading mistake needs slower question analysis. A process mistake needs step-by-step practice. A pressure mistake needs timed practice and calmer routines.

## AI Prompt For Pattern Review

After you have recorded at least five mistakes, paste the tracker into AI and ask:

> I am preparing for [WAEC/JAMB]. Here is my mistake log: [PASTE LOG]. Classify my mistakes into concept, memory, reading, process, and pressure mistakes. Tell me the biggest pattern and give me a three-day correction plan.

After AI responds, do not stop there. Choose one mistake pattern and act on it. If AI says your biggest issue is reading questions too quickly, your next practice session should include underlining keywords. If AI says you are weak in a concept, your next session should begin with explanation and examples before practice.

## Re-Practice Rule

A mistake is not corrected just because you understand the explanation today. You need to answer a similar question later without help. That is why the tracker includes a next practice date.

Use this schedule:

- Review the mistake immediately after practice.
- Try a similar question the next day.
- Try another similar question three days later.
- Add the topic to a mixed quiz at the end of the week.

If you keep missing the same type of question, do not call yourself dull. Change the method. Ask for a simpler explanation, use a textbook, meet a teacher, or practice easier examples before returning to exam-level questions.

## Weekly Score Review

At the end of the week, count your mistake types. If concept mistakes are reducing, you are understanding better. If reading mistakes remain high, slow down. If pressure mistakes remain high, practice with a timer more often. If memory mistakes remain high, create flashcards or short revision notes.

The goal is not to have zero mistakes immediately. The goal is to stop repeating the same mistakes blindly.

## Example Of A Good Mistake Review

Weak review sounds like this: "I got it wrong. The answer is B." That does not teach you much.

Strong review sounds like this: "I chose B because I saw one familiar word in the question, but the question was asking for the exception, not the correct statement. The correct answer is D. My mistake type is reading mistake. Next time I must underline words like not, except, most likely, and best."

That second review gives you a behavior to change. It shows what to do differently next time.

For calculation questions, a strong review may sound like this: "I knew the formula but substituted the values wrongly. My mistake type is process mistake. I need to write the formula first, identify each value, and only then calculate."

For theory questions, a strong review may sound like this: "I memorized the definition but did not understand the example. My mistake type is concept mistake. I need one simple explanation and three examples."

## How To Use The Tracker With Past Questions

Past questions are powerful because they reveal patterns. When you use this tracker with past questions, do not only record the year and answer. Record the topic and mistake type. After two or three weeks, you may notice that some topics keep returning. Those are your priority revision areas.

You can also ask AI:

> Based on these past-question mistakes, tell me which topics I should revise first and what kind of practice I need for each one.

If AI gives a broad answer, ask it to be more specific:

> Turn this into a three-day correction plan with exact topics, question practice, and review tasks.

This is how the tracker becomes a study plan.
